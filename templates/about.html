About Our Anemia Diagnosis Tool

We’re committed to making preliminary anemia screening more accessible by using microscope images of red blood cells and evidence-based analysis.

Our Mission

To provide accessible, reliable preliminary health screening tools that empower individuals to take proactive steps in managing their health. We believe early detection is key to better health outcomes.

Why Anemia Screening?

Anemia affects millions worldwide and often goes undiagnosed. Our tool helps identify potential anemia cases early, encouraging individuals to seek proper medical care and treatment before complications arise.

Evidence-Based

Our approach uses computer vision to analyze red blood cell microscope images. The model is trained on labeled examples to learn visual patterns that can correlate with anemia-related changes, and it’s intended for preliminary screening rather than definitive diagnosis.

Privacy & Safety

Your health data privacy is our priority. Remember, this tool is for screening purposes only and should not replace professional medical advice.

Understanding Anemia

What is Anemia?

Anemia is a condition where your blood doesn't have enough healthy red blood cells or hemoglobin. Hemoglobin is the part of red blood cells that carries oxygen throughout your body. When you have anemia, your body doesn't get enough oxygen-rich blood, which can make you feel tired and weak.

Common Symptoms

  • Fatigue and weakness
  • Pale or yellowish skin
  • Irregular heartbeat or shortness of breath
  • Dizziness or lightheadedness
  • Cold hands and feet
  • Headaches

What We Analyze (Microscope Images)

AnemiaCare analyzes images of red blood cells captured under a microscope. From these images, the model focuses on visual cues such as:

  • Red blood cell color/contrast and overall appearance
  • Cell size patterns and variability across the field of view
  • Shape irregularities and deformation patterns
  • General cell distribution and density in the sample area
  • Image quality factors (focus, lighting, blur) that can affect reliability

For clinical decisions, a healthcare professional may still rely on confirmatory testing (for example, a complete blood count) and clinical context.
